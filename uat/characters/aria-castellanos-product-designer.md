# Aria Castellanos — the design leader who needs the tool to *reason the visa choice*, not pick the default

**Background / lived experience:** Senior product designer turned design lead at a venture-backed
SaaS company, on an H-1B that didn't clear the lottery a second time; the O-1 is now her realistic
path and the clock is real. Her record is a hybrid that doesn't sit cleanly in one box: a **Webby**
win and two **Awwwards Site of the Day** honors (creative/arts-flavored), but also a **design-system
she built and open-sourced** that three other companies adopted, **conference talks** at Config and
an industry summit, a **lead role** on a flagship product, **press** in a design publication, and
top-band compensation. She *thinks* she's an O-1A "business/design leadership" case — that's what a
recruiter told her — but she has read enough to worry that a designer's award/press/recognition
profile is what USCIS literally describes under **O-1B arts**, and that choosing the wrong
classification means being scored against the wrong criteria and eating an RFE. She priced two
firms: one quoted **$9k for an O-1A**, the other pitched her as **O-1B at $7k** — and the fact that
two lawyers disagreed on her *classification* is exactly why she trusts no one's gut, including her
own. She has used ChatGPT and got a confident "you're definitely O-1A" with zero reasoning.

**Voice:** crisp, systems-minded, design-critique register. Talks in trade-offs and rationale —
"what's the reasoning, what did you compare it against, show me the alternative you rejected." She
will accept a recommendation she disagrees with *if the reasoning is sound*; she will not accept a
recommendation with no reasoning, and she instantly distrusts a tool that picked a default.

**Jobs-to-be-done:**
- Get an honest, *reasoned* call on **O-1A vs O-1B vs EB-1A** for a design profile — not a coin
  flip, and not whatever box she happened to tick first.
- See her actual evidence (Webby, Awwwards, the design system, talks, press) mapped onto the right
  criteria for whichever path wins, so she knows where she's strong and where she's thin.
- Get a first-draft petition letter good enough that her attorney refines rather than restarts.

**What good looks like:** the "best path" comparison scores her profile against **every** program
and explains *why* one wins (e.g. "you clear O-1A 5/8 today; O-1B is 3/6 but the arts framing may
fit your award/press profile"), naming her real evidence; if she's on O-1A the criteria shown are
the **O-1A eight** (awards, membership, press, judging, original contribution, scholarly, critical
role, remuneration) and if she switches to O-1B she sees the **different six** (lead role in
distinguished productions, national/international recognition, reviews & press, commercial/critical
success, recognition from organizations & experts, high salary) — not the same labels relabeled.

**Pet peeves:** a recommendation with no rationale; a "best path" that's obviously just defaulting
to O-1A; her Webby/Awwwards silently dropped because the engine only understands "business"
evidence; the criteria list looking identical no matter which visa she picks; invented awards or a
fabricated "adopted by N companies" number; a green "Met" on a criterion she didn't actually
support.

**Motivation (time-saved):** the LLM-less way is **two consultations + $7k–$9k + 6–10 weeks** of a
firm deciding her classification and assembling the packet — and the consultations *disagreed*, so
the real cost is decision paralysis on a visa clock. The app wins if it gives her a reasoned path
call and a usable draft **in an afternoon** for a fraction of the cost. A confident-but-unreasoned
verdict that sends her down the wrong classification is *worse* than nothing — it costs her an RFE.

**Senior-quality bar:** at least as good as a thoughtful immigration associate who, handed her CV,
would *reason out loud* whether the design profile is better argued as O-1A extraordinary ability in
business or O-1B distinction in the arts — and would map Webby/Awwwards/talks/design-system to the
correct criteria for the chosen track. A senior would reject a tool that picks a lane without
justifying it, or that scores a designer against business-only criteria and quietly loses the
arts-flavored evidence.

**Scored acceptance criteria (applied identically every run):**
1. [ ] The "find my best path" output **reasons** the O-1A vs O-1B vs EB-1A recommendation (a real rationale tied to her evidence and thresholds), not a default-to-O-1A coin flip.
2. [ ] When she views O-1A she sees the **O-1A eight**; when she switches to O-1B she sees the **distinct O-1B six** (lead role / recognition / reviews / commercial-critical success / org-expert recognition / high salary) — the packs are genuinely different, not relabeled.
3. [ ] The verdict names *her* real evidence (Webby, Awwwards, the open-sourced design system, the talks, the press) — not generic placeholders — and maps each to a correct criterion for the active pack.
4. [ ] Nothing she didn't claim shows as "Met"; thin criteria (e.g. judging, scholarly articles) are flagged honestly as gaps for a design profile.
5. [ ] The draft cites only facts she supplied — **zero fabricated** awards, adoption numbers, talks, or press — and crosswalks each section to the correct criterion of the chosen classification.
6. [ ] The not-legal-advice `DISCLAIMER` rides on every AI payload (verdict, best-path, draft) and she's clear her attorney must review/sign — the tool reasons the path but never *decides* her eligibility.
7. [ ] Time from paste → reasoned path call + usable first draft is plausibly an afternoon, and the share link reads as a credible verdict she could send an employer or attorney.
